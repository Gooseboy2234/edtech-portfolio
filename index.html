Task 10 · Final Campaign

"Screens with Sense"

Turning the research into a message a real parent can act on

Overview

For the final task I designed "Screens with Sense," an evidence-led Instagram campaign aimed at one real-world persona — Amber, a 29-year-old single mother in Cartagena who works in IT and sees educational technology as a "slippery slope" that can help or harm. The campaign translates the meta-analytic evidence from Task 9 into a warm, visual message that affirms her instinct to guide rather than ban: monitor, guide, don't ban. It pairs four campaign slides with a written rationale and a comparison of how three AI models read the same evidence.

The Campaign

A four-slide arc moves Amber from fear to action — open with the panic she already feels, replace it with the consistency of the research, show that how screens are used matters more than how long, then hand her one concrete habit.

Who It's For

Persona
Amber
29 · Cartagena
Single mother, works in IT. Limits her own screen use and reads educational tech as a slippery slope — able to help or harm. Believes screens and "natural" tools should work in harmony, and trusts her own judgement and the parenting voices she follows online. The campaign meets her there: an Instagram presence that guides rather than bans.

Rationale

The dominant school discourse frames screens as a threat to be restricted — a fear "Screens with Sense" deliberately challenges. Drawing on the evidence gathered in Task 9, it reframes the conversation: across 182 studies and 1.1 million young people, the link between ordinary screen time and harm is consistently small. What matters is not the hours but the habits — when, how, and with whom screens are used.

That evidence becomes messages Amber can act on. The clearest, most reliable finding — screens before bed disrupt sleep — becomes a single concrete habit ("screens off one hour before bed, charged outside the bedroom"). The nuance that active, co-viewed use beats passive scrolling becomes "watch, play and talk together." The campaign reinforces Amber's harmony mindset and her appetite for credible, practical guidance while challenging the panic narrative without dismissing her caution — building a bridge to informed dialogue: monitor, guide, don't ban.

Reinforces

Harmony of tools · guided, monitored use · trust in practical, credible advice

Challenges

"Screen time = harm" panic · ban-first thinking · hours over habits

AI Comparison

Three models, one question: what does the evidence say about screens and youth? I asked ChatGPT, Claude, and Copilot the same question and compared how each read the research.

ModelCore stanceEmphasis & sourcesBlind spot / bias
GChatGPT Habits over hours. Ordinary screen time has only a small effect; compulsive use, poor sleep and content drive harm. Notes screens can also help. Sleep as the most consistent pathway; bidirectional effects. Leans on recent large meta-analyses (182 studies / 1.1M; 117-study longitudinal). Balanced — the most even-handed; few obvious gaps.
CClaude Hours matter most. Treats total time as the leading factor. Stresses harms are larger for adolescent girls and for younger children. Agrees sleep is the most consistent finding. Cites a mix of old and recent studies, yielding more inconsistent results. Focuses on negatives; under-weights the benefits of screen use.
CoCopilot Why it's used matters most. Findings are consistent but small; the reason for use is decisive — educational use can flip harm into help. Bedtime use especially harmful; gaming & compulsive use worst. Provides its own effect-size table; leans on recent reviews. Closely echoes ChatGPT — little independent challenge.
Synthesis
  • Where all three agree: bedtime screen use is the most consistently harmful factor — the single safest thing to act on.
  • Where they split: ChatGPT and Copilot say how screens are used matters more than how long; Claude treats raw hours as the leading factor.
  • Why it matters: the models drew on different source bases — Claude mixed older and newer work, the others leaned recent — which explains the disagreement.
Taken together: digital media can harm, but monitored use and a few habit changes reverse most of that — so guided, watched screen use does more good than harm. This is the evidence the campaign is built on.

Reflection

The hardest part of this task was resisting the urge to "win the argument." The research gave me an easy headline — the harm is small — but Amber doesn't need to be argued out of her caution; she needs something she can do on a Tuesday night. Building the campaign around one habit instead of a statistic taught me that translating evidence is its own skill: the goal is a behavior, not a citation. Running the same question through three AI models also showed me how much a source base shapes a "neutral" answer — the models disagreed not because the evidence was unclear, but because they'd read different slices of it. That made me a more careful consumer of both research and AI. [Edit this into your own voice.]

How this connects to TPACK / PIC-RAT

This campaign lives at the TPACK intersection: the content knowledge (what the screen-time research actually says), the pedagogical knowledge (how to change a parent's behavior without lecturing), and the technological knowledge (using Instagram's visual, carousel format as the medium). On PIC-RAT, the campaign pushes the audience from Passive consumption of panic headlines toward Interactive, Creative habits ("watch, play and talk together") — and aims for Amplification / Transformation of how families use screens, not mere Replacement of one warning with another.
